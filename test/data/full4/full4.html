Urban Delights

Although not renowned for amazing scenery and pristine rivers, the Central Belt should not be written off as a white water venue. Much favoured by city dwellers catching an evening paddle, and offering an option for students and others who always have limited petrol in the car, these rivers feature some nice paddling.

Often you will have to compete with shopping trolleys and abandoned washing machines for space in the eddy and the colour of the water may prove a tad worrying, but for the dedicated enthusiast armed with their nose clips, a lot of fun can be had! In wet conditions, the Clyde and Kelvin offer some ‘interesting’ playboating and on both the Gryfe and Mouse Water, there is a drop or two that will give you something to think about.

264 Avon

 

Contributor Douglas Rae

Grade 2/3

Length 6km

Start NS 954 735 (55.9437, -3.6749)

Finish NS 982 772 (55.9765, -3.6312)

Introduction

Flowing through Linlithgow, this grade 2/3 run is worth a go for anyone in the Central Belt area. Considering its location, the water is quite clean (classed as moderate by SEPA in 2008) and the river is surrounded by pleasant forest with a nice nature walk beside the river.

Gauge

If it chucks it down in the Edinburgh area for a few days, then this one should be running. At least 1.0 on SEPA gauge Avon (Forth Valley)@Polmonthill is best.

Access

Put in at the bridge where the A801 crosses the Avon. Safe parking can be found by turning left directly after the bridge (coming from the north) and driving a short distance along the road. It is also possible to put in higher up.

Take out in Linlithgow just after the bridge. Get out at the slalom training site by the Bridge Inn.

Description

The river consists of mainly grade 2 and 3 rapids and gets better further down in a sort of half gorge section. The only major hazard is a bizarre little weir that drops straight into a grade 2 rapid. The river gets more powerful with a lot of water.

265 Almond

 

Contributor Tom Crow

Grade 2/3(4)

Length 7km

Start NT 076 678 (55.8945, -3.4776)

Finish NT 105 705 (55.9198, -3.4329)

or NT 119 727 (55.9395, -3.4116)

Introduction

Flowing from the outskirts of Livingston through Almondell and Calderwood Country Park, this is a surprisingly decent, intermediate grade run which flows relatively frequently.

Gauge

Generally wet weather is required; if the ground is wet then it doesn’t need much rain to get it into condition. Above 0.8 on the Almond (Lothian)@Almondell SEPA Gauge is a runnable level.

Access

Put in at Almondell and Calderwood Country Park Mid Calder car park on the Linhouse just above its confluence with the Almond. A footbridge crosses the Linhouse Water at this point and makes an easy access point.

Egress can be made either at Lins Mill Aqueduct, which requires a steep and slippery climb back up to the Union Canal on the left bank, or by paddling 2km further downstream to the stone bridge in Newbridge.

Description

Putting in above the confluence with the Linhouse Water leads straight into a wide section of river above a low vertical weir. This can be run hard left at lower levels (shallow) but forms a nasty hole when higher, portage right. Below this the river narrows with wooded banks before the canal feeder weir is reached – a rough shallow slide. A couple of nice grade 2 rapids follow flowing beneath an old railway viaduct before the SEPA gauging weir is reached, marked by the hut on the right bank and cable across the river. This can be a bit
retentive at high flows but makes a nice playspot at lower levels.

The river steepens on the approach to the next bridge with a series of ledges and chutes both upstream and downstream. A small suspension bridge marks the start of a nice long section of grade 2/3 with a mixture of shingle rapids and boulder gardens. After approximately 3km the Lins Mill Aqueduct and the Union Canal come into view and the final grade 3 section begins. Here the river bed becomes bedrock and the gradient steepens, forming a series of wide playwaves at most levels, getting bigger and steeper below the aqueduct. At higher flows, the holes become big and retentive with the section culminating in a pushy river-wide ledge approximately 2m in height (Gd 4). Egress can be made here by walking up the left bank and climbing up to the canal or continue below the M8 down to Newbridge.

Other important points

The Almond collects the treated outfalls from several sewage treatment works and is rated poor water quality by SEPA, so it’s probably worth trying not to drink too much water,
especially immediately after heavy rain.

There’s a short section of grade 4 on the
Almond at Cammo just upstream of Cramond Brig. A narrow bedrock fall, it runs even at quite low levels. Downstream, between Cramond Brig and the Firth of Forth are two large weirs. The first is rocky and shallow with no clean line. The second, marked by a ruined mill on the river right, is a vertical fall of 2m with a slight horseshoe shape creating a nasty hole at most flows.

The Linhouse Water has been run (Gd 4) from the A71 culvert (NT 074 660; 55.8786, -3.4801) down into the Almond. This is a tight and technical run of small falls and slides through a lovely wooded glen. The tunnel at the put-in can be run but the dog leg makes it dark and sketchy with protection recommended at the ledge two-thirds of the way through. Take care for fallen trees. It needs the Almondell SEPA gauge to be 1.2 or above.

North Esk

Rising in the Pentland Hills above Penicuik,
the North Esk has nearly 25km of paddling all within a stone’s throw of Edinburgh.
Roslin Glen, the middle section, is a particular highlight as the river flows through a stunning sandstone gorge alive with wildlife and steeped in history. Sections above become an option at higher flows. Of moderate grade throughout, with a couple of harder falls and weirs and several surf waves, for those in the Lothians this makes a great after-work run or quick blast for those short of time.

266 North Esk

 

(Upper)

Contributors Tom Crow and Jonathan Riddell

Grade 3(4)

Length 7km

Start NT 235 595 (55.8233, -3.2211)

Finish NT 271 628 (55.8531, -3.1649)

Introduction

The upper section runs from Penicuik to Roslin.

Gauge

Esk (Lothian)@Musselburgh SEPA gauge: 0.6-1.0 is low; 1.0-1.5 is medium; above 1.5 is high.

Access

Access is normally straightforward and without issue though be aware of people’s gardens and private land when paddling through the several small towns. Some of the roads to the access points are extremely steep and narrow.

Description

Starting downstream of the A701 bridge in
Penicuik fast water leads to what is probably the hardest rapid on the whole river (Gd 4). An old railway bridge signals the approach: watch out for tree blockages in the bridge arches. Climb out river right above the bridge to inspect
as gabion baskets line the bank downstream making an exit very difficult above the falls. Bedrock slabs with big holes in high water lead to a river-wide ledge; river right normally provides the best line.

Downstream, low-hanging trees and fast shallow water lead to the bridge at Auchendinny, where alternative access can be made. A long sloping concrete weir is run in the middle before a nice long section of grade 3 boulder gardens continues past the new housing development at Dalmore. Continue beneath the old arched viaduct to the recently collapsed gunpowder mill weir. This can be paddled through the new channel on the right, or portaged on the left through the ruins. Fast water with a couple of playwaves continue to the road bridge at the start of the mid-section at Roslin.

266 North Esk

 

(Middle)

Contributors Tom Crow and Jonathan Riddell

Grade 2(3)

Length 5km

Start NT 271 628 (55.8531, -3.1649)

Finish NT 288 648 (55.8714, -3.1384)

Description

From the put-in at Roslin, the river sweeps round a right-hand bend on the approach to the gorge. A grade 3 rapid leads to a narrow slot which can be tricky and needs inspecting for wood. Below the slot you enter Roslin Glen, a deep, dark, incredibly scenic sandstone gorge. Keep an eye open for wildlife and for the castles and ruined bridges high up on the lip of the gorge. Fun, straightforward rapids continue
with several undercut cliffs to watch out for. At higher flows, a nice wave forms directly beneath a waterfall coming off the right wall. Once past a huge rock face on river left the bedrock walls recede, replaced by steep wooded banks. Landslides are common here and can change the nature of the river dumping wood and debris in the main channel. A steeper
section with overhanging trees leads to another
large concrete weir near Springfield. This weir started to collapse in early 2018 and will
undoubtedly change further. It is currently
unrunnable, with the main flow running through a ragged breach on river right. Portage on the left. This is followed by a short fast section before the road bridge at Polton is reached. Access/egress river left, downstream of the bridge.

Other important points

Despite its moderate grading, this is not a run for beginners, due to the numerous dangers such as low trees, wood, undercuts and weirs. The car park at Roslin glen closes after sunset. SEPA rated the river quality as poor; try not to drink it.

266 North Esk

 

(Lower)

Contributors Tom Crow and Jonathan Riddell

Grade 2/3

Length 6km

Start NT 288 648 (55.8714, -3.1384)

Finish NT 327 672 (55.8938, -3.0775)

or NT 338 722 (55.9389, -3.0605)

Description

The lower section runs from Polton to Dalkeith and thence to Musselburgh. The weir downstream of the put-in should be inspected carefully. The river left side has recently (2018) completely collapsed allowing a clear route but it may collect wood. Big bouncy water leads beneath an old railway viaduct to Lasswade, which is an alternative access/egress. Downstream, easy and scenic paddling leads through the grounds of several country houses. A large weir, immediately downstream of a single span arch, should be inspected. From here it is a straightforward run though to Dalkeith where egress can be made at Ironmills Park.

The scenery gets grander on the final 8km to Musselburgh, which takes you into Dalkeith Country Park and the tax haven owned Buccleuch Estates. There is a vertical weir after the impressive Montagu Bridge: take out before the bridge to portage on the right. Grade 2 rapids take you through to the Meeting of the Waters with the South Esk where the river gets wider. A 45° weir as you enter Musselburgh can be slid down in the centre to finish your journey.

Other important points

The Water of Leith can be paddled, but be careful to inspect the weirs.

267 Mouse Water

 

Contributors Kenny Biggin and Tom Crow

Grade 3(5)

Length 3.5km

Start NS 904 452 (55.6885, -3.7439)

Finish NS 877 449 (55.6852, -3.7870)

or NS 869 441 (55.6777, -3.8000)

Introduction

The Mouse Water runs through a combination of flat farmland and steep-sided wooded gorge. It could be classed as a two-hit wonder
but in reasonable flows the section in-between
is quick and enjoyable enough, and the two hits good enough, to make the whole run worth doing.

Gauge

As with most hereabouts, this river definitely
needs rainfall, more so since the dam was constructed. When you look from the put-in bridge at the pool way below, you’ll get a pretty good feel for whether its high or not. If the falls here are obviously separate tiers with plenty of rocks showing but you can still see a line worth running, then the trip is just about on – though the sections between the two main falls will be scrapey.

Access

Park on Hagholm Road, cross over the bridge and climb down to the put-in. Take out at Mousebank Road bridge or at the confluence with the Clyde at the caravan park.

Description

A short grade 3+ rapid leads to the first set of falls which, at grade 5, are arguably the best and the hardest on the river. A hydro scheme has been installed on the lip of the falls which diverts a small amount of flow to the power house at the base of the falls. This has two effects: the flows are tempered somewhat which means the falls run more safely when the rest of the river is high (which you want). However there is now a river-wide weir right on the lip of the falls. This makes the initial entry awkward and, with a sharp concrete edge, isn’t great for the plastic of your expensive creek boat. Be warned!

There follows a nice twisting gorge with nothing too hard. With a lot of water, the gorge is good fun and makes the river worth doing for folk who don’t fancy the first drop. The second notable set of rapids is about halfway down after several twisty gorge sections with plenty
of tree jam possibilities. To scout the falls
(Gd 4-), eddy out right opposite a large rock wall on the left – clamber steeply up the right bank beside a tiny stream and onto the path from where you can walk along and have a look. The section down to the lower take-out is in a spectacular wooded gorge and is pretty good if there is plenty of water. There is nothing too serious, but it does let you finish on a few rapids rather than the flat that leads up to the first take-out.

Other important points

Beware of trees, there are often many stuck across this river, a saw may be useful.

268 Clyde

 

 

 

 

(Falls of Clyde)

Contributor William Austin

Grade 3/4 (5+)

Length 4km

Start NS 891 404 (55.6447, -3.7635)

Finish NS 867 439 (55.6753, -3.8027)

Introduction

The Clyde is one of Scotland’s larger rivers so often has water when smaller rivers don’t. At New Lanark, it goes into a gorge and over four waterfalls. From upstream, these are Bonnington Linn, Corra Linn, Dundaff Linn and a few kilometres downstream, Stonebreyes Falls. The gorge is one of the most impressive big volume gorges in Scotland and well worth checking out at most levels.

Gauge

There is a hydro scheme just before Bonnington Linn which takes the water out before the gorge and puts it back in below Corra Linn. In low to medium water, this often makes the level in the gorge appear different to the level on the river below. However, from a higher medium it tends to balance out. If the hydro scheme is operating you’ll need about 1.1 on the SEPA gauge for Abington to do the whole run including the gorge. If the hydro system is open but not switched on, the level is more like the natural flow and you can paddle this section from about 0.55.

Access

A small road leads to the hydro at Bonnington. You can’t get all the way down as there’s a gate a few hundred metres before the dam. The parking on this road is only big enough for one or two cars.

The take-out is at the New Lanark Visitor Centre (NS 878 426; 55.6641, -3.7840) or at Kirkfieldbank Bridge on the A72 Glasgow/Hamilton to Lanark road. Park by the old bridge. A path and steps lead to the take-out, river left, about 20m down from the new bridge (NS 867 439; 55.6753, -3.8027).

Description

For most, this run is split into two sections, from Bonnington to Corra Linn then Corra Linn to New Lanark. However, if you’re feeling particularly brave then you’ll be happy to know that everything in this section has been run before, including the 25m Corra Linn. Although it’s technically possible to paddle the section with no portages, please remember that there is a good reason that Corra Linn has only been run a handful of times!

Paddle from the put-in to the dam at Bonnington Linn. Portage/inspect the dam and falls on river right. At 10m, Bonnington Linn is a relatively tall but straightforward drop, if run hard left. Be careful if running any other lines; there are a lot of slabs under the water making parts of the pool extremely shallow! If you don’t fancy Bonnington Linn, a path/scramble to the pool below starts by the ruins of a small bridge. From here, grade 3+ rapids lead to Bruce’s Cave (Gd 4). At lower levels there’s an undercut, river right at the bottom of this rapid. After Bruce’s Cave, flat water for 50m or so leads to a sharp right-hand corner; this is the take-out, unless you want to run the falls! Take out on river left, where there is a short scramble up a steep bank which then leads to the footpath beside the river. Not far down the path there is a viewpoint for the falls. As you are walking into this viewpoint there is a hidden path on the left-hand side behind the railing. This leads down to the pool below the drop. You can easily see the first part of the path as it’s well used and goes to an unofficial viewpoint, but about 5m into the path look left into the trees and you’ll see a faded track. Follow this down to the water.

Paddle up to base of the falls, then its grade 1/2 down to the power station outfall. Flat water leads down to the intake weir for New Lanark. Take the weir on the right in high water, or left in lower conditions. Some 300m of grade 3 rapids lead to Dundaff Linn (Gd 3+). This 3m fall can be run anywhere. Hard left is the easiest as there are number of small steps, however any line is good to go; only note that most of the drop lands on a slab, so make sure you put a good boof in! Float on grade 2 water past New Lanark. A good playwave forms at the outfall from the New Lanark hydro scheme. You can either get out here and climb up the wall into the visitors centre or continue on mainly grade 1 water and get out at Kirkfield Bank.

Stonebyres Falls is 2km downstream; it’s a double drop followed by a tall slide. As you come up to it, portage around the hydro dam on river
left. While out of your boat it’s well worth looking out for an eddy on river right that you can scout the drop from. The double drop is great fun if you’re on the right line with plenty of water. However, it is quite the opposite if it’s too low and you’re in the wrong place! There’s a narrow slot and a bad undercut on river left which you want to be as far away from as possible. Run the falls on the right. After the double drop, you have 20m before the main slide. This is both tall and technical. About half way down the slide, most of the water crashes into the wall on river right. To avoid taking a big impact, or even getting pinned, on the lip aim your boat at about 10 o’clock and put a huge right stroke in. It has a high chance of not being particularly pleasant; I would recommend elbow pads and a full-face helmet!

(Crossford)

Contributor Robin Cole

Grade 2/3

Length 5km

Start NS 843 442 (55.6775, -3.8401)

Finish NS 827 468 (55.7007, -3.8674)

Introduction

Although the Clyde may be paddled from
Lanark to Glasgow, the best of the paddling lies upstream of Glasgow, close to Lanark. This very popular section below Stonebyres makes a good intermediate trip.

Gauge

This section is generally paddleable, except after dry spells of a week or more. A reading of 0.5 on the Clyde@Hazelbank SEPA gauge should be considered a minimum flow. The playwaves at Crossford are excellent with 1.0 on the gauge.

Access

Park in the A72 lay-by and cross the fence to the path down to the river. At the take-out park in the public car park (NS 825 467; 55.7000, -3.8699) and walk down the drive, in the corner of Holm Road, to the river.

Description

The main features are the obvious rapids at Hazelbank and the excellent waves/stoppers below Crossford Bridge. A footpath on river right allows inspection of the more difficult sections.

269 Nethan

 

 

 

Contributor Gavin Millar

Grade 3/4

Length 3km

Start NS 813 456 (55.6896, -3.8888)

Finish NS 824 470 (55.7022, -3.8725)

Introduction

The Nethan is a tight run with a committing gorge.

Gauge

The river goes at 0.8 or above on the Nethan@Kirkmuirhill SEPA gauge.

Access

Park the car on the wide section of road at the steep tight bends and walk through the Holmhead Farm field to the weir. Take out at the A72 Road Bridge in Crossford.

Description

From the get-in, there is a 1.5km stretch of constant grade 2 water which provides an excellent warm up until the riverbanks begin to steepen. With Craignethan Castle looming high above, a small bedrock weir creates a tame river-
wide stopper and marks the entrance to the gorge. The river turns sharply right with the first small fall immediately around the corner (Gd 3), a 1m bedrock shelf with broken rocks at the base and an overhanging tree. Shoot left down the ramp.

Constant grade 2/3 waves and stoppers follow for the next 400m until the river again turns sharply right, and immediately over a fall (Gd 3+). Most of the flow heads towards a large strainer on river left so an easier route could be the chute on the right but watch for overhanging trees protecting the entrance. The large waves and holes below are good fun. Continuous grade 2/3 leads swiftly to the next bedrock shelf (Gd 3). This long diagonal shelf has a hole on the right-hand side. Shoot left over a shallow 1m slide.

The gorge then begins to get narrower and more intimidating with several large grippy holes and a distinct horizon line. Make sure you pick an eddy in plenty of time to get out and have a look, as a wrong line on the next fall, Hades Gate (Gd 4), could be severely punished. The essence of this drop is a long bowl-shaped slide, with a large hole in the centre. The best line is hard river right which offers a clean slide but you have to work hard to get to it. There is an option of a shallow slide river left, but there’s a large boulder near the base and another strainer to the left. A swim on this would need swift rescue as the next fall, Death on a Stick, follows after 50m of confused and messy water with several large boulders strewn across the river. A challenging grade 4+, with tight and twisty drops through huge boulders which have fallen from the gorge walls, river right looks to run best. Grade 2 leads swiftly to the A72 road bridge and the get-out.

Other important points

Watch out for the trees, there will be strainers. Don’t try to climb out of the gorge on the left if you get stuck.

270 Avon Water

 

Contributors Robin Cole and Andrew Bell

Grade 3(4)

Length 11km

Start NS 750 474 (55.7039, -3.9892)

or NS 752 508 (55.7347, -3.9883)

Finish NS 733 546 (55.7687, -4.0208)

or NS 733 569 (55.7895, -4.0217)

Introduction

The Avon is about 40 minutes drive to the south of Glasgow near Larkhall. The lower section passes through the spectacular Chatelherault Park and joins the River Clyde at Strathclyde Park. The river flows through steep wooded gorges, made up of sedimentary rocks. This means that there are a few obvious narrows and the river is semi-continuous, particularly in the lower section.

Gauge

There needs to have been a wet spell and preferably rain the night before paddling this one. The upper stretch is narrower and may still be OK when the lower section is scrapey. The best gauge is at Larkhall. If there is enough water running down the river left of the big weir to avoid scraping your boat too much, the upper section will be quite good. A reading of 0.8 on the Avon Water@Fairholm SEPA gauge should be considered a minimum flow for the upper section, while 0.6 on Avon Water@Fairholm is the minimum for the lower. If the short half-width concrete weir just below the put-in is covered, the lower section is worth doing. If the island is covered, the river is in spate.

Access

Put on at the bridge outside Stonehouse for the upper or at Larkhall for the lower section. Egress either at Strathclyde Park, or drive towards Hamilton on the B7078/A72 and turn left just after the bridge over the Avon.

Description

After putting on at Stonehouse, the river gradually builds up to give two fun grade 4 rapids: the aptly named Boulderfield, followed by a 3m chute, The Big Lynn. The 5m high weir at Millheugh (Larkhall) marks the end of the upper section and needs inspecting and probably portaging on the left of the fish ladder. It has been run down the vertical right-hand side. A new fish ladder was constructed in 2016 but repairs are ongoing in 2017 and it has not been known to be paddled. When repairs are finished it may provide an alternative line, but watch for the zig-zag currents and pins at low water.

From Larkhall, the lower section builds up to give continuous grade 3 paddling. At the entrance to the gorge, the river takes a right-hand bend. Some 200m below this, there is a straightforward rapid with a tricky full-width stopper hidden at the bottom. The best rapids are those near the end, directly below the high road bridge at Chatelherault. The final weir at Hamilton can be shot hard right. It has another new fish ladder with the same potential and caveats. At higher flows (above 1.2 on the gauge) get out river right well above the bridges which mark the weir at the bottom of the lower section; getting off the river below the bridges can be problematic.

271 White Cart Water

 

Contributor Neil Farmer

Grade 3/4(5)

Length 10km

Start NS 579 534 (55.7531, -4.2646)

Finish NS 585 601 (55.8138, -4.2591)

Introduction

An urban run in Glasgow with an unusual feel, there’s a fairly big rocky waterfall in the middle to portage.

Gauge

This run needs water flowing over the road ford at the put-in, and the waterfalls at the end in Linn Park need to be paddleable. They can be found by walking upstream through Linn Park on the river right bank.

Access

The put-in is on Holehouse Road, just north of the A726 main road; it’s where the road fords the river. The egress is at the bridge at the end of Snuff Mill Road. For a shorter trip, access can also be made river right just above the A727 bridge in Busby (NS 579 565; 55.7811, -4.2658).

Description

When you pass under the A727 road bridge near Busby, by a park with a steep bank on the river right, take care as you are approaching a big waterfall on a right-hand bend; the entrance shelves gradually drawing you in (NS 579 567; 55.7828, -4.2661). These falls have been paddled at high flows, river right (Gd 5), but come with a health warning as they are rocky and shallow at the base. A path can be followed from the A727 bridge, river left, to inspect and portage. The waterfall in Linn Park is a vertical shelf drop of about 2m, and is a nice place to practice boofing.

Other important points

Significant care must be taken in the middle section: it would be very easy to accidentally paddle over the large waterfall as it isn’t that obvious from above. This would probably not be much fun. Take care with potential construction/rubbish in the riverbed.

272 Kelvin

 

(Upper)

Contributors Robin Cole and

Andrew McLaughlin

Grade 2

Length 3km

Start NS 555 706 (55.9075, -4.3131)

or NS 552 703 (55.9043, -4.3171)

Finish NS 561 687 (55.8905, -4.3018)

Introduction

The Kelvin runs through the West End of Glasgow, and despite its urban setting is a surprisingly good trip.

Gauge

A reading of 1.0 on the Kelvin@Dryfield SEPA gauge is a good introductory level.

Access

Get on at the entrance to Glasgow Golf Club in Bearsden or the university sports grounds downstream. Take out at Kelvindale Court.

Description

The top section wanders through Dawsholm Park and offers a good novice section with numerous small rapids and surf waves. If there is any flow be sure to get novices out (river right) before the big “V” shaped Kelvindale Weir (NS 562 688; 55.8912,-4.3002) as this gets very nasty at high flows.

Other important points

Weil’s disease has been reported on the river; don’t drink the water.

272 Kelvin

 

(Lower)

Contributors Robin Cole and

Andrew McLaughlin

Grade 3/4

Length 5km

Start NS 561 687 (55.8905, -4.3018)

Finish NS 557 659 (55.8654, -4.3073)

Introduction

Between Great Western Road and the confluence with the Clyde there is a waterfall plus up to seven rapids, depending on the level of the tide, most of which have surfing potential. The unbroken weir at the put-in should be treated with caution. Like most urban rivers it is best not to paddle the river immediately
after a long dry spell because of the risk of Weil’s disease.

Gauge

A reading of 1.0 on the Kelvin@Dryfield SEPA gauge is a good introductory level.

Access

You can reach the Isle of Arran by catching the ferry from Ardrossan in Ayrshire to the main ferry terminal in the town of Brodick, or from Claonaig on the Kintyre peninsular to Lochranza at the north end of the island. For ferry crossing and timetables go to www.calmac.co.uk .

This is a gem of a steep burn that leaves you wanting more. The burn demands respect in high flows, so inspect on the walk up and get your camera out ready for action. The main section is normally run from an obvious double drop just inside the forest tree line. Put in above the double drop, Nut Cracker. The first 4m drop is steep and split into two channels; run on river right into the plunge pool and then you’re straight into the second 6m slab with a cushy landing, giving you enough time to catch your breath. If the river is chocolate brown and in flood, it would be wise to scout all the drops on the walk-in to assess how feasible it is. Introduction

The Whiteadder is the most scenic river in the Borders, offering something of interest to most types of paddlers. When it rains heavily on the Lammermuirs, a gorge section from the attractive estate village of Abbey St Bathans provides some exciting white water. Further downstream, a more gentle paddle can be found.

Gauge

This river runs low the majority of the time, and heavy rain is needed for an enjoyable paddle. A gauge in feet from 1-6 is found at Abbey St Bathans ford. Below 1 and the river is low, 2 is at least medium and 2.5+ is high. Over 4, the river becomes much more serious. There is a SEPA gauge at Whiteadder Water@Abbey St Bathans: 0.8 is a minimum flow.

Access

The put-in is at Abbey St Bathans’ ford next to the ‘River Café’ (NT 763 619). There is plenty of open space and access to the river is straightforward. Parking for the take-out can be found in a rough area of ground on the west side of a bridge crossing the Whiteadder, 1½ miles west of Preston (NT 774 577).

Description

The Whiteadder flows gently from Abbey St Bathans. When you pass the Retreat House (a unique circular building tucked away on the left) the water becomes more challenging with a brief continuous grade 2/3 section. As the next rapid grows closer, watch out for a 2nd century fort and broch, located high up on the hills to the right. Calm water leads to a couple of bigger drops, the second of which might be considered grade 4. In low water protruding rocks obstruct the main channel on the left. The river now relaxes until Elba. A footbridge and a right bend mark a 1m fall. From here the valley opens out, and straightforward, shallow rapids lead to a ford built over pipes, which should be portaged.

Other important points

High water may result in fast flows and tree blockages. This run is generally shallow and pins are a potential danger. The scenic half-day trip from Dexter’s Bridge on the Duns-Chirnside road to Canys also comes well recommended. This includes several small rapids, drops and caulds.

307 Teviot

Grade 2(3-)

Length 5km

Start NT 481 134 (55.4126, -2.8213)

Finish NT 532 168 (55.4429, -2.7397)

Introduction

The Teviot is a tributary of the River Tweed, joining the larger river in the historic town of Kelso. In its upper reaches, the river resembles something of an urban battleground, particularly during the fishing season, with overhanging trees and attendant shopping trolleys. Further downstream the river mellows, even if some of the anglers don’t, and gradually
meanders eastwards to join the Tweed. If you don’t want to talk to the fishermen, consider paddling on Sundays or out-with the fishing season (which runs from April to November).

The section described is a good run to take
beginners after they have a few trips under their belts – a good step up from the Tweed. The river provides challenges without being too difficult, but still has enough to keep more experienced paddlers from crying with boredom. In addition to the grade 3-, there are numerous bouncy grade 2 sections with some surfable waves as you go down.

Gauge

The bigger, the better, for this one. The Teviot is runnable all year round, although if there has been no rain for a while it is a bit scrapey, especially when running the weir and on the section before the Slitrig joins the Teviot. The SEPA gauge Teviot@Hawick should read at least 0.4.

Access

Put in at the side of Martin’s Bridge, near the A7 at the B711 turn-off, about 1 mile south of Hawick. Just over the bridge is a car park with room for about four cars; this car park is used by walkers so there may be limited room. The take-out is about 1½ miles below, downstream of Hawick near the Riverside Caravan Park. Take the slip road marked Glenteviot Park
Hotel on the upstream side of the caravan park.

Description

Straightforward water leads from the put-in to an island just above the rugby pitches. Stick to the right-hand side where there is more flow as the other routes have bushes and trees in them. As you enter Hawick a short paddle on flat water leads to a long smooth weir with a salmon ladder in the middle. Running the ladder adds a bit of excitement for more experienced paddlers, or take a safer route down either side. A chute over a small weir is followed by a bend and you are now presented with bushes growing in the river – stick with the main flow to the right. The river then turns another sharpish corner and you are into the Horn’s Hole section (Gd 3-). Bouncy waves and a few stoppers with some rocks to dodge run on down to a small fall that disappears into a wave at higher levels. The take-out is just after this above the stone bridge. It isn’t the easiest take-out point in the world, but the current here is slow and it’s shallow near the bank.

Other important points

This trip can be combined with a run on the Slitrig if the rivers are high. It joins the Teviot in Hawick centre just after the first weir and creates some interesting currents.

Introduction

The Yarrow is a sheltered grade 2/3 trip with an attractive gorge midway down. If the majority of rocks are covered at the put-in, and the river at this point looks easily navigable, then the trip shouldn’t be too rocky. There are two put-in points for the Yarrow. The first is in Yarrowford, which is located about 6 miles up the Yarrow valley on the A708; you’ll find a car park and phone box here. The river narrows and disappears round a bend, warning of a slightly harder drop (Gd 3+), which may merit inspection. Below the fall, mid-stream rocks catch the unwary, particularly at high flows when they could be partially hidden. This is the end of the gorge and after paddling under an old bridge the river banks drop to reveal the surrounding countryside.

From here it is only a short stretch of calm
water until the river joins the much larger Ettrick Water. This too is generally calm other than the initial cauld, which is easily portaged, and some interesting waves which appear when the river is very high. The take-out at the swimming pool comes up about 300m below the main road bridge.

Other important points

Watch out for strainers! The banks are heavily wooded and fallen trees can become jammed in the gorge. Overhanging branches may also obstruct the flow and restrict access to the bank. There are ropes attached to the gorge walls, which cause a major hazard in high flows. Check the SCA access reports for latest updates on trees.

Description

The higher put-in gives access to a grade 3/4 gorge, but this stretch is heavily overgrown and probably not worth the risk. Starting from the usual put-in, a sharp right-hand bend into a narrow 4m slide, Right Hand Slide, marks the start of a gorge. This is followed by two 3m drops, the second of which has a twisty inlet rapid. About 10m after the second drop, the gorge briefly narrows, making a chunky hole when there’s a lot of water. After this hole it is relatively simple, gorged, grade 2/3 for the next kilometre, with the odd grade 3+ rapid, until you get to a small technical 2m drop, which in lower flows is divided by a rock. Run the right channel when rock is visible. Grade 4 water follows for the next 500m with one particularly horrendous sharp left-hand turn, Left Slot Stopper, which almost looks impassable in lower flows.

You then pass under a big tree across the top of the gorge and you are in to Moffat Well Falls. This double drop is probably the most technical feature on the river. Run the first one just right of centre and then immediately get your boat pointing fully left to make the line river left on the second drop. The line is literally as wide as a kayak! Immediately after this, there is a third drop which can get blocked by trees; make sure you inspect before running it.

A mix of grade 2–3 follows for 1km or so down to the portage where the river is too narrow for a kayak. More grade 2 water leads down to Kayantics HQ where they have built steps to climb out.

314 Evan Water

 

Contributors Max Twomey & Dave Colvill

Grade 3(3+)

Length 6km

Start NT 048 066 (55.3442, -3.5026)

Finish NT 078 027 (55.3103, -3.4541)

Introduction

This river offers continuous enjoyable grade 3 paddling and is well worth doing. Considering how handy this is for Glasgow and Edinburgh paddlers it’s surprising that it isn’t better known. Although the motorway is never far away it is hardly noticeable, especially when on the lower half. It combines well with the Kinnell Water which is just around the corner – both tend to run at the same levels.

Gauge

At the take-out, look at the rock on river right upstream of the bridge: the river should be at least level with the top of the rock to make the trip worthwhile. The SEPA gauge for the Kinnel Water@Redhall is a good guide: 1.0 is the minimum.

Access

Exit the M74 at J15 and go into Beattock. The take-out bridge is by Lochhouse Farm B&B; there is a large parking area in front of the building. Before setting off for the put-in, take a good look at the bridge you are on, there are so many on this stretch it is easy to get out at the wrong one! Then head up the B7076 to a railway arch signed Middlegill and park considerately.

To access the river, cross the road; there is a short track leading to a farm bridge and the river. If you want to inspect the gorge stop on the way up by the white square building with the satellite dish on top (NT 078 027, 55.3103, -3.4541), cross the bridge and walk upstream on river right.

Description

The run is quite straightforward at the start, beginning with a couple of kilometres of fun read and run water leading to the tunnel. This runs under the road but is less intimidating than the one on the Kinglass. Watch out for the wall that runs midstream along its length. Below the tunnel an obvious horizon line signals a largish fall (Gd 3+), which you may wish to inspect. It generally runs but the stopper in the centre can be large.

More grade 3 water follows, including a pleasant sandstone gorge, announced by a large fallen
tree which is easily paddled over. The gorge contains a larger drop with a choice of channels, Three Choices (Gd 3+); the middle seems to go best. Not far beyond is Zigzag (Gd 3+). Make sure you get out at the correct bridge.

315 Kinnel Water

 

Contributor Dave Colvill

Grade 3(4)

Length 6km

Start NY 065 958 (55.2475, -3.4710)

Finish NY 068 921 (55.2150, -3.4663)

or NY 059 901 (55.1962, -3.4798)

Introduction

Best considered the Evan Water’s bigger
brother, this river makes a great alternative to some of the better-known runs in the Borders and deserves to be paddled more often. Excit-
ing but never desperate, it’s great boating when there’s lots of water.

Gauge

Like the Evan this needs a ton of water to get it going. Look over St Ann’s bridge; the river should preferably look brown and bouncy with few rocks visible on the downstream side for a good trip. Use the SEPA gauge for Kinnel Water@Redhall; 0.9 m is the absolute minimum for paddling with levels of 1.3 and upwards ideal. At higher levels, the river is committing and scarily fun!

Access

From St Ann’s bridge head north on the A701 for about 1½ miles to a small turning on the left. Follow the road down to a paddock, some signposts for anglers and a small car park. Put in at the pool just above the wooden bridge.

There are two options for the take-out. The first is to drive towards Dumfries for ½ mile from St Ann’s bridge and take the first left to Hartfield farm. Park considerately half way down the farm track. The alternative, which is only really worth it if the river is in spate, is to extend the paddle for 2km and get out at Courance. To get here, go south on the A701 for about a mile then take the small road on the left signposted ‘Lochmaben’. Follow this road until you see a lodge house on the right-hand side of the road with a large estate house behind it. It’s a good idea to walk down to the river at both get-outs so you can recognise where to egress when you come to it.

Description

The first rapid at Boreland Bridge gives a general taste of the river down to the gorge; really
nice fast grade 3 alternating with flatter sections. Often the trees hang down over the rapids restricting possible lines and providing traps for the unwary. The river carries on in the same vein – as the banks close in and heighten,
a metal footbridge appears. Keep an eye out for a sharp left-hand bend with a fallen tree about 300m above the gorge, a telegraph pole close to the left-hand bank is pretty well the last chance to jump out and look (best inspected from the left). Mark your route down the double drop and boily S-bend, then go for it, safe in the knowledge that at grade 4, this is the most difficult part of the river. If you’ve missed the last eddy, centre left then hard right seems to work.

Once past the gorge, fast water leads to St Ann’s bridge and an eddy to catch your breath for the final rapid. At lower levels, this consists of a succession of four or five grade 3 drops. In higher water, these form large stoppers – the final one is a tricky twisting affair. For those that have parked at Hartfield, watch out for the ruined building on the right-hand bank which marks the get-out. The river now begins to flatten out to shingly grade 2 as the walls recede and the second get-out approaches on a scenic and relaxed bit of water. Watch out for log jams on this stretch.

Other important points

Fallen trees are a possibility on the whole river and there are lots of sweepers and strainers along the way.

The river is fished, but probably not at the
water levels needed for paddling. The locals seem friendly, and considerate parking etc. will keep it this way.

About a kilometre into the run, an exciting looking tributary, the Duff Kinnel Water, enters on river right. The bottom 500m has been paddled and is grade 4; the rest may well be worth a go.

Contributor Gordon Ross

Grade 1/2(3)

Length 10km

Start NY 362 846 (55.1524, -3.0016)

Finish NY 394 765 (55.0797, -2.9489)

Introduction

Flowing from Davington across the border and into the Solway Firth, this trip is a true cross-border raid. Surely Scotland’s most southerly river! The Esk offers several different sections suitable for beginners.

Gauge

The level can be checked from the road. A reading of 0.8 on the SEPA Esk (Solway)@Canonbie gauge is the minimum recommen-
ded level and 1.1 is a sweet level for beginners.

Access

The river runs close to the road with several access and egress points either side of Langholm. For a grade 1 trip, drive north from Langholm on the B709 for approx 9 miles. There is good access and adequate parking where the river first crosses the road at Enzieholm Bridge. Alternatively, several minor roads provide access to the river, and for a shorter trip, turn right onto any one of these. There are possible access/egress points at Bentpath Bridge, Burnfoot Bridge, Potholm Bridge and finally Langholm Bridge. Access at Langholm Bridge itself is good. Driving south along the A7, access to the river is possible at Skipper’s Bridge (after about a mile), or turn left to reach the river at Hollows or Canonbie.

Description

Starting at Enzieholm Bridge the river is grade 1 as far as Langholm. The short stretch from Langholm to Skipper’s Bridge is also grade 1, with the exception of Skipper’s, a 300m grade 3 rapid near the end of the trip. In the event of a mishap, the pool below the bridge allows sufficient slack water for rescue. Egress to the B6318 can be made at this point.

Dog Island is the site of the next major rapid (Gd 2/3). It is normally tackled on the right-hand side. However, it is important to cross to the left before the final run into the pool, avoiding a nasty rock and ledge on the right. The next island just after Irvine House should be paddled on the right (Gd 2/3). In low water, watch out for a large rock half way down the rapid on the right-hand side. From here, the river is again grade 1, with the exception of Hollows Weir, just above the Hollows Bridge. After Hollows Bridge a large rock on river right signals an excellent playspot with a great surf wave in very high water and an even better hole for loops etc. at anything above 1.5 on the gauge.

Other important points

The River Liddle, which is close at hand and forms the border with England, has a nice grade 4 section on it at Penton Linn (NY 430 773; 55.087, -2.893).

Contributor Tom Botterill

Length 4km

Start NY 334 825 (55.1323, -3.0461)

Finish NY 362 846 (55.1524, -3.0016)

Introduction

The Wauchope Water runs alongside the B7068 Langholm-Lockerbie road and joins the Esk in Langholm, at the top of the classic section.

Gauge

This river comes up after a few hours of heavy rain. Look over the road bridge as you come out of Langholm. If all the rocks are covered and you could float down it will go.

Access

The river is visible on your left as you drive out of Langholm towards Lockerbie on the B7068. Go over a bridge and drive a couple of miles up the road. Put on where the river comes close to the road. Take out in Langholm or continue down the Esk (which will be high).

Description

This is a good alternative start to the Border Esk if it’s been raining. It starts with a few easy rapids and lots of tree dodging. After a kilo-
metre, there is a 1m drop with a powerful hole, followed by a smaller drop (Gd 3). You can see this from the road as you drive up. 30m below this there is a barbed wire fence that needs to be portaged. A kilometre of easier water brings you to the road bridge, which is followed by a longer grade 3 rapid. There is a 1m sloping weir as you come into Langholm. About 500m of easier water leads to the take-out in Langholm or you can continue on to join the Esk.

River Names and Pronunciation

By Ron Cameron

River and place names in Scotland come from a variety of languages spoken here at one time or another. Many come from Gaelic which was widely spoken except in the South East. Norse names are common especially in the north and the islands. Brythonic names (Pictish and Welsh) are ancient and widely distributed. Anglian, Scots and English names dominate in the South East but are common elsewhere. Some names come from really ancient pre-Celtic Indo-European base languages. River names are usually very old and may not resemble any modern language. Although hazardous to attempt a translation, as names often come to resemble something completely different, the meanings I offer here are either direct translations, likely meanings or copied from published sources. For more translations and a full list of the sources used, visit www.andyjacksonfund.org.uk

Phonetic guides to Gaelic pronunciation are approximate as the Gaelic language uses a variety of sound that are not present in English. In the phonetics guide ch is always as in loch, rather than church. Ron Cameron is solely to blame for the phonetics, which are shown below in square brackets, [phonetics].
