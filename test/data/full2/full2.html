Fort William Area

From a paddler’s point of view, Fort William is very much the capital of the Wet West Coast of Scotland. With rainfall recorded here on an average of 250 days per year, it’s easy to see why. The rivers here are generally spate runs which are quick to rise and fall, sometimes frighteningly so! If you’re unlucky and find dry conditions then perhaps Glen Spean to the north or Glen Etive to the south will be a better bet. When the rains come and the rivers rise the white-water paddler is spoilt for choice. Go scare yourself on the Upper Nevis or Allt Mhuic, or try a more controlled trip on the Loy or Arkaig. The more adventurous might try Ardgour and beyond for some wilder, more remote, runs. There is something here to suit all tastes. In the town itself, you’ll find plenty
of choice for food and drink. There is also a reasonable selection of outdoor gear shops (sadly no paddling shop currently), an excellent climbing wall and a swimming pool with sauna
for easing off the shoulders after a long day of bashing down borderline unrunnable ditches! With its hospital and hectic (!?) night life, Fort William itself caters for all eventualities.

101 Allt Camgharaidh

Contributors Andy Jackson and

Kirsten Rendle

Grade 4(5)

Length 1km

Start NN 014 905 (56.9633, -5.2679)

Finish NN 021 916 (56.9734, -5.2574)

Introduction

Tucked away on the ‘wrong side’ of Loch Arkaig, the Allt Camgharaidh is one of three rivers that drain into the loch at its western end. A short fun trip with the odd hole to keep you on your toes and a great drop to finish, it is well worth the trip up the tortuous road from the milk run on the River Arkaig.

Gauge

No tiny burn, the Allt Camgharaidh should hold its water reasonably well, and is worth a peek after a period of moderate rainfall. You will need to get a feel for how wet the day is from elsewhere. The best you can manage is to peer across the loch, ‘Um’ and ‘Ah’ thoughtfully, then toss a coin to decide if the paddle across the loch will be worth the effort.

Access

From the put-in for the River Arkaig, drive west for what seems like forever, on the minor road beside the loch. At Rubha Giubhais, just before the road climbs away from the loch, you will find limited parking in a lay-by. It’s a little under a kilometre to paddle across the loch, then a 1km walk up, best on river left. The tributary that cascades into the river on the far bank marks the most obvious put-in. The fit and keen could walk higher.

Description

The river has a beautiful setting. It runs through an unspoilt and seldom visited glen, where remnants of Caledonian pine forests provide a taste of Scotland long since gone. The river is not super steep, and you’ll be able to appreciate the beauty of the surroundings.

From the put-in (at a sketchy looking drop by the tributary) you are straight into the upper gorge. This is tight and fast and there isn’t much space for more than three boats. Grade 4 at lower levels, it gets harder with more water. Remember when you’re inspecting on the walk up, that everything looks smaller from above! The gorge opens out, but the fun’s not over; watch out for a slightly bigger drop with a sticky hole at the bottom.

Slides and drops continue below this. The best is saved till the end, with the last drop falling into the loch itself. This is Grizzly, a grade 5 with a tight line to avoid the cave behind the fall, or an easy portage on river left. The waters of the loch welcome you at the bottom; a great way to finish the trip. Well not quite, you still have to paddle back across the loch.

Other important points

The upper reaches of Glen Arkaig hide several other small creeky runs. Go explore.

102 Allt Mhuic

Contributors Andy Jackson and Calum Hogg

Grade 4(5)

Length 500m

Start NN123 921 (56.9822, -5.0894)

Finish NN 119 912 (56.9738, -5.0947)

Introduction

A great little river with a nice series of clean drops followed by a slightly more adventurous section. Pack the elbow pads.

Gauge

The Allt Mhuic can be run in any decent-sized spate, but might be a little scrapey unless there’s been a good bit of rain. The level shoots up and down in no time at all!

Access

Follow directions for the put-in for the River Arkaig. You will find the river 4 miles further along the Loch Arkaig road where there is a small Forestry Commission car park. The best access is up the river left bank. Walk up until you get to the bridge over the first big slide. Put in river left upstream of the bridge.

Description

The river starts with a steep slide which launches you straight into a clean double drop. From here you can enjoy a good few slides and clean drops. All too soon a big double drop appears and you will want to make sure you find a suitable eddy in good time; there is a nice eddy on the right immediately before the drop. This 17m double drop has been run but be sure to have a quick sanity check before committing: not one to mess up! It has an awkward start on the river left, and requires a solid boof to ensure a flat landing as the pool on the second drop is not very deep. After this the river gets a bit more sketchy, but offers some exciting drops, including a gnarly double through a natural arch; be sure to boof on a left hand stroke to avoid denting your boat/paddles/head/general structural integrity on the tight landing before heading through the arch. Stick right to finish this one. If in doubt, take your time to scout or portage as things can creep up on you quickly!

103 Mallie

Contributors Andy Holt and Andy Parley

Grade 4(5)

Length 500m

Start NN 095 872 (56.9374, -5.1315)

Finish NN 139 889 (56.9543, -5.0605)

Gauge

This river needs a day of moderate rain to get it going. At more than a medium level, the upper gorge will be a serious undertaking.

Access

The Mallie is on the south west side of Loch Arkaig. Drive up the loch for about 2 miles then paddle across. Walk up a good track for about 4km and put on at a flat section before it steepens out of sight.

Description

The best action starts straight away. The river
steepens from the put-in and you are into grade 3 territory with small drops, twists and turns for about 150m. Two grade 4 rapids, with good eddies on river left, precede a bigger drop. All will require a scout. The waterfall, Hide and Seek, is about 4–5m and runs at grade 4+. There is a last chance eddy on river right above, and a straightforward line river left. Watch out for the powerful hydraulic at the bottom and a cave behind the fall. You can protect the fall from river right above. The next mini gorge is a sweet grade 3+ technical section for about 100m.

After this, the river relaxes and runs at grade 2+ with the odd playwave to keep you entertained for 2km before you come to a bridge. Directly under this is the final grade 3+ section, a ramp with a hole to punch before a boily slot that bends round to the left. A couple more twists and turns and it’s back to the loch to tap into your reserves for the paddle back.

104 Allt Chia-aig

Contributors Andy Jackson, Andy Burton and

Length 2km

Start NN 181 908 (56.9726, -4.9928)

Introduction

The Allt Chia-aig is a tributary of the mighty River Arkaig, situated close to Fort William just north of Spean Bridge. Described as a true ‘Highland epic’ by those foolish enough to paddle this burn, the Allt Chia-aig is more of an adventure than an everyday kayaking trip.

Gauge

Sadly, there is now a hydro scheme on the river,
so it may no longer be worth the effort. The water returns at the very end of the run, above the final fall by the road so the flow here can be misleading and you may require more water than you think for the rest of the run. Don’t be put off by this cascade; some of the run is a little nicer!

Access

See directions to the Arkaig. Park in the Forestry
Commission car park just before you cross the Allt Chia-aig and head up the forest walk which leads steeply up through the trees. At the forest road, turn left and continue up the glen. When you can’t be bothered to walk any further and the river upstream appears to be flattening off, cut down through the forest, aiming to get on below a large ugly fall (the entrance to the Alamo Gorge).

Description

The first descent of this river had much to do with a dry spell in Fort William (in itself an unusual event), on a drunken night in the Commando Bar and far too much testosterone floating around. All that you need to know about this river can easily be gleaned from a few quotes from the epic that followed:

“If I ever get out of this, I’m going to kill you.” “Oh my God, I need a shorter boat.”
“Ouch.”
“He just went down!”, “Yeah, which way?”
Thump.
“This isn’t !?!?!?!?!? canoeing.”
“Remember the Alamo.”
“Did anyone bring a climbing rope?”
“That was a nice drop, what’s that doing on this river?”

Other important points

Surprisingly this run has had several descents and even return visits. The lower half of the final fall can be played on as a park & huck, which adds a bit of excitement to an Arkaig trip.

105 Arkaig

Contributors Alastair Collis and

Bridget Thomas

Grade 2+(4)

Length 2km

Start NN 169 887 (56.9541, -5.0109)

Finish NN 187 877 (56.9452, -4.9818)

Introduction

A considerable river, the Arkaig drains a wild and remote mountain area close to Fort William. It links Loch Arkaig to Loch Lochy. The splendour of both lochs can be enjoyed from the put-in and take-out. The river offers only one difficult rapid, which can be portaged. The rest of the run has some surf waves, giving an entertaining if short paddle; ideal if time is tight or you are looking for a second or even third run of the day. If you are paddling along the road at the get-on, buckle up for the most exciting trip on the Arkaig you’ll ever have.

Gauge

Looking upstream from the road bridge at Bunarkaig (NN 187 878), the last rapid can be seen. If this rapid is mainly rock with only one line, river left, the run will be low. No rocks here and the run will be high. The Arkaig rises slowly, due to the size of Loch Arkaig. In the past it has been worth a visit even several days after heavy rain, but with the construction of a dam taking water from the main event, this may no longer be the case.

Access

The Arkaig can be reached from just north of Spean Bridge, by turning onto the B8004 at the Commando Memorial. After crossing the Caledonian Canal, turn right and follow the signs to Bunarkaig. The obvious road bridge shows the last rapid. Parking is available at the take-out located beside the loch, some 200m before the bridge. To reach the top of the river, follow the road and turn left at the next collection of houses. Cross the Allt Chia-aig and park at the loch end.

Description

From the put-in, paddle the short distance along the loch and under the road bridge. The pillars create some good eddy lines to warm up and play on. Heading on downstream, the river is flat until you come to a left-hand bend. A new dam was built here in 2016, which looks very dangerous at most levels. The developers have promised remedial works to make the weir safer, but at present it looks like a potential death trap when the river is high. A new portage path has been put in on river left at the lip of the dam, from which you can inspect the dam and the Main Event, the only major rapid of the trip which lies just beyond. Caution is required at high flows as the eddy here is not very big. It’s possible to portage the dam and get back in just below to run the Main Event. With lots of water, this rapid is quite impressive with big rolling holes and an alpine feel. Check out both sides of the island; if the river is high you may be tempted to carry back up to run the other side as well. A new surf wave has formed downstream of the outflow from the hydro scheme, which at some flows is very playable and eddy serviced. There are more surf waves downstream but most are ‘one shot wonders’. The last rapid above the bridge comes all too soon. It is an easy grade 3 in all levels of water. You’re now on Loch Lochy – the SCA agreed egress point lies just along the loch shore on river right.

Other important points

The Arkaig is a run that obviously winds its way through someone’s back yard. The banks are beautifully tended and ornate paths wind alongside the river to a summer-house. Please keep noise to a minimum, respect your surroundings and you will be made very welcome.

106 Loy

Contributors Rory Stewart and

Bridget Thomas

Grade 3/4

Length 2km

Start NN 125 831 (56.9017, -5.0796)

Finish NN 149 817 (56.8898, -5.0390)

Introduction

The River Loy flows south-east out of Glen Loy and into the River Lochy, some 3 miles north of Fort William. It is a short spate river, with a number of rapids and small drops, which provide great entertainment. Narrow and tree lined, fallen trees can end up in the river! The single-track road up Glen Loy is adjacent to the river bank for most of its length, making it easy to scout on the way to the put-in.

Gauge

This run needs lots of rain. Not altogether rare in these parts, but the relatively smaller catchment area of this river makes it slower to come up than the likes of the Roy. There’s a gauge painted on the downstream side of the B8004 road bridge. At 1, the river is just high enough, although the wider bits will be shallow and bumpy. Below this level you are signing on for a geology field trip. At 2, the river starts to flex its muscles and one or two moderately retentive features appear. A reading of 3 or above is fun for strong, confident paddlers. Rescues will be difficult as the river flows through the trees and the eddies disappear. At this flow the river should be considered a solid and continuous grade 4.

Access

To reach the river, follow the B8004 (the back road between Fort William and Spean Bridge, through the village of Banavie) until you cross the river at an obvious humpbacked bridge. Drive up the road beside the river for a little over 1km and get on beside a large gravel lay-by where the river is flat. If you prefer a longer warm-up get on at the next bridge at Inverskilavulin (NN 125 831) or continue on to the end of the tarmac road. Take out upstream of the B8004 road bridge.

Description

If you start at the end of the tarmac the river is low volume and bouldery with a couple of bedrock rapids. The small island should be passed on its right-hand side. Look out for the remains of an old dam and beware of river-wide strainers from adjacent plantations in this upper section. From the bridge at Inverskilavulin, the first kilometre provides a good warm-up. A series of small rapids leads to 3 drops, which can be inspected on the drive up. The largest has 3 chutes, and is best boofed down the middle one; the landing is fairly shallow. The river eases briefly, before the action picks up once more. After a right-hand bend, the next ledge goes on the far right; watch out for an awkward midstream boulder just over the lip. Further downstream, a grippy pour-over is best avoided, although at some levels it can provide good entertainment for the brave and the unwary. The last rapid offers a choice of routes. Take out river right above the B8004 road bridge.

In 2016 a salmon-counting trap was placed across the width of the river below the road bridge. This was soon washed away leaving just tangled remains, but may be reinstated and is scheduled to be in the river for the next few years. If the salmon trap is not there when you paddle the river, you may be tempted to continue downstream, through the tunnel under the Caledonian Canal. This is a great fun way to end your trip and is highly recommended. At very high water levels, headroom in the tunnel might be limited! Return to the road via a small side tunnel river left.

Other important points

Parking at the take-out is a bit limited, so please try not to obstruct the narrow road. Chris Pedley reports some good steep grade 4 action at the head of the glen if levels are really high. Walk up a kilometre or so from the road end.

107 Lochy

(Fort William)

Contributor Mags Duncan

Grade 1/2(3-)

Length 10km

Start NN 176 839 (56.9109, -4.9968)

Finish NN 121 754 (56.8327, -5.0809)

Introduction

The River Spean becomes the Lochy at
Mucomir Power Station and flows sedately to Fort William. This run provides a gentle introduction to moving water. At very high flows this river becomes a different animal; expect huge waves and some munchy stoppers.

Gauge

The river always holds water, but can become fast in spate conditions. You can check the flow at the put-in. The first shingle rapids give a good indication of the trip; 0.6 on the SEPA gauge should be considered a minimum level.

Access

The best put-in is half a mile downstream of Mucomir Power Station, on the back road from Spean Bridge to Fort William, B8004. Alternatively for a shorter trip, put in at the take-out for the River Loy, and paddle through the tunnel under the Caledonian Canal (watching out for the fish counter). Take out just before Fort William at Inverlochy. Driving south towards Fort William, turn right off the A82 just past the junction with the A830 opposite the petrol station (sign-posted to Old Inverlochy Castle). Take the first right and cross the outflow from the aluminium factory. Just past the castle ruins there is a large car park for Stramash Nursery.

Description

The run consists mainly of grade 1/2 shingle rapids with one grade 3 on a left-hand bend at Eas nan Long (The Waterfall of the Ship), more commonly referred to as Torcastle. At low water, this rapid is a straightforward chute into a pool. With more water it becomes a powerful rapid with several alternative chutes, surging boils and powerful eddy lines. Take out river left, after the road bridge, and above the railway bridge.

Just downstream is the outflow from the
aluminium smelter. Depending on the river level and the state of the tide, this tailrace can provide a fine playspot but is more normally a boily mess. The river has a tendency to collect tree debris which may become a hazard, particularly at high flow.

Other important points

It is possible to make a round trip by paddling up the Caledonian Canal from Neptune’s Staircase, and portaging into the river at Gairlochy; possibly not the most exciting white water-option!

108 Allt Daim

Contributor Alastair Collis

Grade 5

Length 2km

Start NN 177 744 (56.8252, -4.9884)

Finish NN 167 757 (56.8368, -5.0063)

Access

It’s a 5.5km walk to the start of this run, unless you can get permission from the Forestry Commission to drive the first 3km. Park at the Nevis Range ski area and then walk 3km up the forest road. After that there is a further 2.5km walk to the top of the river. As the smelter intake dries the lower half of this burn you really need permission to use the Forestry Commission road (not to mention a key to the barrier) to make this run feasible.

Description

Some classic slab paddling is on offer here. The first slab is a bit messy but the second, found 500m downstream, is a real classic! From here on, there is a mix of bedrock and boulders. A shallow drop forces a short portage where the river enters a mini-gorge. It gets steeper towards the end. Take out at or before the
diversion weir.

109 Allt a’ Mhuillin

Ben Nevis Burn

Contributor Richard Bannister

Grade 5

Length 800m

Start NN 147 752 (56.8311, -5.0387)

Finish NN 141 756 (56.8351, -5.0500)

Introduction

For those who like it steep: 200m of descent in 800m, this run is a bit like being poured down a rocky gutter – strangely satisfying.

Gauge

A lot of water is abstracted into the aluminium smelter aqueduct at the put-in, so it needs to be wet for this one. The burn should be just bank-full, not raging, at the BP petrol station as you leave Fort William travelling north on the A82.

Access

On the A82 north of Fort William, turn right at Torlundy and right again to the North Face car park. Follow the well-graded forest tracks up the path towards Ben Nevis to the small intake dam. Egress below the final waterfall at the footbridge.

Description

Initially the stream runs through a bouldery bed and is too small to paddle. After a while it starts to cut more of a channel and you can put on where you choose for a fun game of rock dodgems. Purists and those with new boats will carry further down, or just run away in tears. The first steeper section cuts into the bedrock with some ‘teacup’ drops – seal launch in river left halfway down. The bottom teacup is not much wider than a boat is long so might take some manoeuvring … be sure to boof out of here! After a flatter section the real fun begins. The gradient ramps up even more for a series of flumes, falls and slides, which amazingly all go. You may need to remove some wood, and watch out for any canyoning gear or participants. When the gradient relents there are two further jagged rapids that may go at some levels, then a picture-perfect clean 4m drop under the footbridge – lovely.

Other important points

This creek has the distinction of draining the highest ground in Britain – the northern cliffs of Ben Nevis. Plus, it finishes at a whisky distillery. It is well worth walking in to the egress to inspect the bottom section before committing yourself to the carry uphill; you can huck the bottom drop at most levels.

110 Nevis

(Upper)

Contributors Alistair Collis and Calum Hogg

Grade 4/5

Length 3km

Start NN 173 692 (56.7784, -4.9917)

Finish NN 145 684 (56.7703, -5.0376)

Introduction

The River Nevis flows through a spectacular glen and on into Loch Linnhe at Fort William, the centre of white-water kayaking on the west coast. At a high flow, the Upper Nevis is one of Lochaber’s test piece grade 5 runs: a rite of passage, giving entry into the ’big boys league’. There are few places on the run where you can afford to allow your concentration to slip from the task in hand and, until you are at the bottom of Polldubh Falls, your thoughts are constantly on what’s coming up next.

Gauge

Typical Lochaber weather will bring the Nevis into good condition. The river comes up incredibly quickly, and in the frequent local monsoons it is often at or above a runnable level! In fact, if it’s raining hard, the level may change dramatically while you’re actually on the river. During the autumn and spring the Nevis runs frequently, but unfortunately not for long. It can be raging one afternoon, then dry the next morning.

One option to judge the level is to look up Meall
an t-Suidhe, the hill at the foot of the glen, in front of Ben Nevis. If two or more of the ‘gauge burns’ are running then the river could be at a good level. Driving up the glen, it is easier to judge the flow once at Polldubh Falls. There should be enough water to float the easy stretch above the falls: no rocks showing at all and you are in for an exciting time. If it looks likely you won’t be able to control if you go down the left channel or right, maybe head elsewhere. A reading of 0.75 on the SEPA gauge should be considered an absolute minimum level and 1.2 should be considered the ‘ideal’ height, where everything is in prime condition. The higher it gets the more likely you’ll be cleaning your river shorts when you make it to the bank!

Access

The Glen Nevis road is obvious from the roundabout by the Woollen Mill at the north end of Fort William on the A82. The road winds up the glen, crossing the river once at Polldubh Falls (NN 145 684). The take-out is just below these falls on river right. There is a large car park just before the bridge, and a few spaces right beside the fall. To reach the put-in, continue on up the glen to a car park at the end of the road. From here it’s on foot with your boat on your shoulder, following the path towards Steall
Falls. After 400m you cross a side stream via some steps cut into the rock. Turn off the trail onto a small path that leads down towards the river. You will arrive at the foot of the Steall Gorge, which is totally unrunnable. Get on as high up as you dare, it’s worth it.

Description

Attempting a description of the first rapid, Boulder Blast (Gd 5), is useless. Suffice to say that it is probably the most continuous rapid of its grade in the area, with 300-400m of continuous carnage. The recent rockfall doesn’t appear to have changed things much. Several
lines are possible but a swim will hurt. To
give an idea of the length and complexity of boulder blast, a good line could be – start right, boof hard, stay right, boof hard, go middle, then left around the boulder, then back right of the island, boof twice, then left, then breathe.

From here the river is open and fast flowing, carrying you quickly to the start of the top gorge, signalled by the water pipe on river left. This gorge gets progressively harder and ends with Dave’s Hole (Gd 4+). Here the water pushes right, feeding you nicely into the hole and the ride of your life. In good levels it is best to hit the hole with speed on the right! At lower levels it can be avoided by heading left.

The river now eases a little but beware, the infamous Scimitar Gorge (Gd 5) is soon upon you. If you have any sense you will have found this from the road and memorised both your run-in and line through the gorge, or your take-out eddy. It is a place that demands respect, with 3 difficult drops in quick succession. Note well the dangerous sump, which lies on river right on the 2nd drop. If you need to be told the line in this maelstrom of water, you shouldn’t be here! Once out at the bottom, don’t let the euphoria take over, you are not out of the woods yet!

Immediately after Scimitar you are faced with a multiple-choice test in The Narrows/Constriction. A couple of routes are available; boats have been stuck in a few of them! After this is Paddy’s drop, which lies under Paddy O’Hearn’s Bridge. In higher levels go left, in lower go right. Try to avoid the dodgy pocket on the left at the bottom of the hole, as it’s difficult to get out of and there is a siphon.

Other important points

At high flows it is worth checking the eddy above Scimitar Gorge before getting on if you plan to scout or portage it. Spid’s Slot isn’t recommended, and if you are here and backwards you’ve made a serious mistake!

Glen Spean

The Spean is by far the biggest river in this ‘neck of the woods’. It offers paddling at all water levels. In even the driest conditions you can enjoy navigating the narrow bedrock channels of the Spean Gorge and in times of extreme flood there is plenty of action to be had on the Upper Spean as the Laggan Dam releases its watery load. The many tributaries of the Spean also provide great runs. The Roy is the most popular, and deservedly so, but check out the Treig and Cour, fantastic in the right water

Many of the north-flowing rivers plus the Spean itself were harnessed by a major hydro-
electric scheme in the 1920s to provide power for the aluminium smelter being built in Fort William. This comprises two large dams on the Spean and the Treig, plus the main tunnel,
which was until 1970 the longest water-
carrying tunnel in the world, at 15 miles long. What this means for the white-water
kayaker is that these rivers generally need a lot of rain or snowmelt to run. Furthermore the classic intermediate trip, the Pattack, up until now free-running, is currently having a dam installed in its headwaters. Whether we will see scheduled releases, as promised, remains to be seen.

This is a tiny creek, suitable for small groups only. Bank support is recommended as there is hardly room for two on the river at the same time. The current situation with wood blockages is unknown. A couple of hours with a handsaw in the summer was enough to clear things sufficiently when we ran this.

Roy

The Roy is one of the busy rivers on the west coast due to its classic rapids and easy road access. The river splits neatly into three sections, the upper, the gorge and the lower. There is also some good paddling on a couple of tributaries, and the ‘upper upper’ for the adventurous. The river is slow to rise, but will be runnable for a few days after heavy rain. The best time to try the Upper Roy is during spring and autumn downpours. Glen Roy was carved by the glaciers that once covered this part of Scotland. Their retreat left behind ‘parallel roads’ visible on the sides of the glen. Ask any local boater
about them and you will no doubt be told about the Roman invaders that started to build a road around the glen from either end. The two teams of slaves missed each other in the mist and the first dual carriageway was made. The real story of how they were formed is on a visitor information board in a small car park on your way to the upper put-in.

Glen Etive and Glen Coe

Surely the home of Scottish steep creeking, Glen Coe and Glen Etive offer between them some of the finest burn bashing to be found in this guide. To get the most from this area you will need to like it steep, if not deep, and be up for the odd bit of shouldering your boat.

The waters of the Leven and Coe offer excellent and challenging grade 4/5 boating while the many tributaries of the Etive feature some fine rock bashing action. That said, the falls on the Middle Etive are not as daunting as they look and the upper and lower sections mean that there is something here for everyone. In times of low water, the Middle Etive is usually
scrapeable but the area comes into its own when the small burns are running. In very high flows, try the Lower Coe, the Coupall, or perhaps some of the burns surrounding the Kings-
house: you’ll have a blast.

As well as the kayaking, the whole area is an adventure sports hub with summer and winter climbing, skiing, hillwalking, mountain biking and scuba diving! The variety is endless and that is in addition to the incredible history of the area. Glencoe, a tremendously beautiful and atmospheric place, was the site of the clan massacre of the Macdonalds by the Campbells in 1692, history well worth investigating.

Glencoe village has all the amenities for the travelling kayaker and the Clachaig Inn boasts a good array of beers and whiskies. There are ample campsites, bunkhouses, B&B’s and self-catering chalets in and around Glencoe to suit all pockets, and wild camping is permitted in parts of Glen Etive, although campfires are not.

Looking at Pinball it’s easy to see how this rapid got its name. Beware of the flipper rock that diverts the water to the right as this has claimed several cag sleeves and elbows. If the river is high, a second line opens up down the left, which is hard to get, but rewarding. The last drop is the Chasm; again there is only one way in. This drop has been run without paddles, as they are (or can be) a nuisance. The ledge out of here is best boofed on the extreme left. “We discovered today that you can avoid falling in the deep muddy sinking puddle of doooooom, after the stile, by cleverly sacrificing your boat and stepping on it as a bridge. Those that have seen the tiny flows on this burn most of the year round will scratch their heads and wonder how this could ever provide worthwhile or efficient power. 174 Falls of Lora

Contributors Andy Jackson and Tim Burne

Grade Playspot

Location NM 911 348 (56.4585, -5.3918)

Introduction

This well-known tidal playspot lies under Connel Bridge, some 5 miles north of Oban on the A85. It forms on the ebb tide as water draining from Loch Etive cascades over a reef in the narrow mouth of the loch, kicking up some stunning surf waves, and at times sizeable whirlpools! When there has been no rain it can be the saviour of a weekend if the tides are playing ball, though can be well worth a trip in its own right.

Gauge

A third-party website has good information on tides http://www.fallsoflora.info/. Roughly, you’re looking for the difference between high and low tide to be over 3 metres, the low tide to be less than 0.7 metres, and to get there about 3½ hours after high tide to make the most out of the session.

Access

Park at the northern end of the Connel Bridge. If driving north over the bridge, take the first right and park in the obvious waste ground/parking area. Walk down the hill from the car park to the inland side of the bridge and put on in the eddy above the falls.

Description

On the biggest spring tides of the year, this site throws up some very large crashing waves, intimidating whirlpools and huge boils. Paddlers with a bombproof roll will have a ball, but note well that this is a more serious place than your average playspot as swimmers are carried out into the middle of the widening channel, and if you get unlucky with a whirly, you can
expect some significant downtime. There are three waves that consistently form.

Starting from the top, the Forever Wave is a wide rolling hump of green water that forms as the water slips over the submerged reef. It is a chilled surf at any time, but with clear
water, a sunny day and the kelp waving at you from the sea bed a few metres below, it’s easy to understand how it got its name. There’s no eddy service for this one, so if you want a second run, you’ll have to shoulder your boat and carry up for round two.

The Main Wave is close to the northernmost bridge stanchion and is well served by an eddy – the current here can get quite strong during spring tides. At big tides, it generally takes the form of a crunchy hole with a large green shoulder on surfer’s right. The munchiness of this hole decreases at lower tide differentials.

On the far side of the Main Wave and 50 metres
downstream is the Ultimate Wave, a surging unpredictable mass that can give awesome rides to those taking the effort to get there (but can equally flush you straight off and mash you in the whirlies). Either drop down to it from the Forever Wave, or cross above the Main Wave, fight your way into the mid-
channel eddy and drop in from there.

Other important points

The wave seems to go through cycles of working well and being ‘broken’. One theory is that it is possibly due to the cycles of kelp growth, but whatever it is, look online for the latest beta to save disappointment.

At the time of going to press (2018) an
experimental tidal power platform has been installed 500m west of the bridge. This is only temporary and apparently safe to craft as the turbines are well below the surface; nonetheless it is best avoided. A welcome development that hopefully will be a success.

178 Orchy

(Middle)

Contributor Alastair Collis

Grade 3/4(5)

Length 8km

Start NN 297 396 (56.5178, -4.7701)

Finish NN 246 326 (56.4529, -4.8482)

Introduction

The Middle Orchy has very defined rapids separated by long flat stretches. Most of the falls are produced by harder sections of bedrock, which have resisted erosion.

With a road running alongside for the upper half of the run, the Orchy is a river where non-paddling partners can watch, and escape can easily be made. Likewise, it makes an ideal group trip since all of the harder rapids can be portaged. For those who prefer to park and play, it is possible to carry up from the new parking area at the bottom of the run, to the potentially feisty playhole above Witches Step – beware in high water.

Gauge

After prolonged rain the river will retain its water
for a day or so. The gauge at Bridge of Orchy helps to judge the flow. There are two metal gauges on the downstream right of the bridge – use the one that goes up to 13. Between 0 and 1 is low, scrapey between the big rapids; between 1 and 2 is medium, good for a first run down; between 2 and 3 is high; between 3 and 5 will feel very big and Eas a’Chathaidh will likely have the central line running; above 5 the river is in flood, rapids will have merged together and the river will have a continuous feel. The road may well be flooded especially in the lower sections of the glen. Don’t forget that during rain there are many small tributaries swelling the Orchy along its length.

About 0.6 on the SEPA gauge should be considered a minimum level for the middle section.

Access

The most regularly used put-in is at the Bridge of Orchy Hotel by the gauge. Another popular access point for more experienced paddlers is where the B8074 first meets the Orchy at the small tributary the Allt Kinglass. There is very limited space for vehicles so please park carefully. For large groups with mini-buses or many cars there is a pull-in 500m upstream of Sheep Trolley Gorge with ample parking. This is the obvious rapid close to the road 1½ miles down the B8074. This also provides an access or egress point for shortened trips. The Forestry
Commission have built a new car park at the end of the run (NN 56.4480, -4.8518; NN 243 320) and riverside path which allows you to walk up to the playhole for some park ‘n’ play and to view the final rapids. The funding package for this project included a grant from the Andy Jackson Fund for Access, using proceeds from previous editions of this guidebook.

Description

Putting in at the Allt Kinglass you only have a short float to get your muscles into boating mode before the first rapid, Big Rock. At grade 3+, this is an ideal warm-up for what is to come below. The next rapid, Chicken Shoot (Gd 4), comes after a right-hand bend and in high flows offers good sport. In low water, the left side of the island is somewhat easier (Gd 3). From here there is a long paddle past the
occasional interesting playspot before the start of Sheep Trolley Gorge (Gd 3+); the old sheep trolley has long since been removed. The wave at the end of the rapid offers
excellent entertainment for flat-hulled boats as well as being a good tail squirt spot. The road is now close to the river all the way to Easan Dubha (essan doo-u), where the river gathers its strength and produces a stiff obstacle (Gd 5-). A run off-line here can go unpunished or end in near drowning! The fall becomes easier with more water, as the middle line becomes a monster wave. However, the consequences of a swim become serious and, unless rapidly ‘bagged’, swimmers may get washed through Saw Tooth (Gd 4) just below. Several people have come out of this experience with scary stories and broken bones.

A small flat section leads to Roller Coaster (Gd 3). Soon after this the river leaves the road. End of Civilisation, by far the best grade 4 and the longest rapid on the river, appears after a left-hand bend. To make the best of this rapid, take a line down the left through the two holes and into the convenient eddy. The second half of the rapid is easier but still demands respect.

Easier water leads to several playwaves and holes, the most inviting of which is only yards from the last eddy before Eas a Chathaidh (ess a’ cha-ay). This is the hardest rapid on the river
and deserves its grade 5 reputation. A large island splits the river giving three choices. In very low water the right offers a 3m vertical fall. At higher flows, this rapidly gains a monster towback and recirculates swimmers very nicely. The left side is more often paddled and offers a double drop that ends in a jagged ramp. In high water, a line opens up over the middle of the island – just don’t get swept right! Portaging is simple on the left on a new path part-funded by proceeds from the previous guidebook. The seal launch entry is fun.

Below here an island splits the river, and at medium/high flows, there is a good but feisty playhole on the left side. This hole can recirculate swimmers at high flows. From here, you are soon at Witches Step (Gd 4-). Here the
river splits again with most water coming down the left. The brave or off-line will drop into the guts of the slot and reappear several metres downstream vacuum packed. Run close to the island on its left-hand side to avoid this underwater experience.

This is an ideal place to end your trip as a short grassy bank leads to the road and Eas Urchaidh (ess oorachay) or the Falls of Orchy lie just downstream. They have been run down the fish ladder (right) in very low water and by the extreme left chute/slab missing the main rapid in medium flows, but the guts of the
rapid only really becomes runnable in spate, and then it is still a full-on encounter well worthy of a grade 5 ticket.

‘Crash and Burne’

Whilst spending a weekend exploring and in search of some lesser run white water, the brutal sounding ‘5km walk-in’ to the Douglas Water piqued our interest. It sounded unlikely many would have ventured up there, but the river sounded worth it. The first significant rapid comprised two chunky drops separated by a short pool. A round of boofs and smiles later and we were half-way done. That’s where the day began to come undone – for me at least. Rob probed first, boofing smoothly right followed by an inelegant bounce down the rocky slab. Feeling on form, I fancied something smoother, perhaps a bit burlier, so opted for the centre line. In retrospect this wasn’t so smooth after all! A kicker of water was made, not by the convergence of two flows of water, but by a small rock.

It’s an easy line. Drift in, line up, melt and tuck. Everything goes black. The next thing I remember? A thunderous white-out world of water covering all but a pocket of air in front of my face – thankfully allowing me to breathe. I had hit the rock, been shunted forward in my boat, hit the cockpit with my head and am now pinned underwater, hazily assessing my predica-
ment. The force of the water on my back is flattening me against my deck – too strong to push myself back into a more neutral position. I manage to wiggle my left leg free, but the right will not budge – it is now the only thing preventing my exit. I hesitantly lift myself into the flow to see what happens, but watch as my knee starts to bend unnaturally the wrong way.

Abort plan. I wiggle some more but only seem to sink further into the kayak. The force of the water rips off my elbow pads, then my helmet. With the helmet floating off downstream the pocket I had for breathing starts to close; I begin to inhale water. “B%!!%cks ...” Back to the plan. Act now, deal with the damage later. I push upwards into the powerful flow and watch as my leg folds 90° the wrong way at my knee, I am caught hanging there for a tense few seconds, then my boat un-pins and my leg is released. I make for the bank. It takes a few seconds for me to realise my lower leg is flopping round like a feeble semi.

Three minutes underwater, two and a half of them unconscious, lost helmet, dignity and ruined ligaments. Entry into the magic knees club complete; and the rapid Crash and Burne gets its name! After four operations and six months of crutches/wheelchair I am back up and just about running again. New ligaments, new limitations, new appreciation of life. I’ve still not been back to the Douglas Water – but I will! From what I’m told (and the photos my team took when they finished the run) it’s a truly spectacular river!’

approaches the normal Kinglas section.

189 Cur

Contributors Tim Burne and Neil Farmer

Grade 4

Length 5km

Start NN 130 024 (56.1776, -5.0135)

Finish NS 113 980 (56.1376, -5.0380)

Introduction

The Cur is a spate run in a remote glen.

Gauge

The Cur runs at similar levels to the Kinglas. Although it can be running OK when the Kinglas is a bit scrapy, it also deals well with high levels (say, if you’ve run away from the Kinglas as it’s a little too high). You should be able to get a good idea of the level from the small bridge at Strachurmore, and decide whether it is worth continuing up the glen.

Access

You can drive as far as the confluence with the Leavanin, where there is space to leave a car. Then you must shoulder your boat for the hike upstream. A kilometre brings you to a notable confluence (NN 130 024), which is a sensible put-in if levels are relatively low. Alternatively, continue further upstream on either fork and explore. If you’re rushed for time, a small bridge at Strachurmore (NN 107 008) marks the end of the white water, but the grade 2 cruise down to Glenbranter is a pleasant enough way to extend the trip.

Description

If the river is high, you can continue further up the river left fork. This gives an open grade 4 rapid leading to a narrowing of the river and a fast-flowing braided section before the confluence. Look out for a fence under the bridge. After the confluence, you soon enter the short gorge above Succoth Farm. This contains the most significant rapid of the run, a ledge drop, which can have a grabby hole in high water. The gorge section for the 1.5km above Strachurmore has several fun sections of grade 4 but is fast flowing and in a forest, so beware of strainers. The majority of the gradient is spent by this point, leaving a pleasant grade 2 float (trees permitting) to the take-out at Glenbranter.

Other important points

An unusual but entertaining one hit wonder (NS 106 967) on the Allt Robuic is not far from the take-out. The burn passes through a tunnel under an embankment. With enough water, you’re fired 15m through a 1.5m diameter tube before emerging into the light and launched off the lip of a 3m drop.

If you’ve got bikes with you and fancy some multisport, there’s a nice single-track MTB loop at Glenbranter!

Length 4km

Start NS 115 815 (55.9891, -5.023)

Finish NS 147 825 (55.9992, -4.9737)

Access

The put-in is just upstream of the Clachaig where a small farm track leads down towards the river. Park in the lay-by opposite the junction and walk your boat 100m down to the river.
Alternatively, there are a number of potential put-ins a little further upstream where the road is close to the river. Take-out at the A815 road bridge where some convenient gates upstream of the bridge on river left bring you up to the road.

Gauge

Some fairly sustained rain is needed to bring this run into condition. The nearby and better known Kinglas should be at a sporting level. If you can get around the first corner without scraping gravel, there should be enough water to make the run worthwhile. If it is brown and flowing fast here proceed with caution!

Description

Shortly after launching, a small weir with a surprisingly grabby hole indicates the start of the gorge. Soon after, the river constricts and squeezes through Gunpowder Gorge, an ugly technical twisting series of falls (Gd 5). The portage, if required, is probably easier on the left through the ruins of the old gunpowder mill. Fun read and run follows until a double step grade 4 rapid under the road bridge in the middle of the run – easy to check out on your drive upstream! The majority of the gradient is spent by this point, with one short section of grade 3/4 before the run eases for a pleasant meander-
ing cruise to the take-out at the road bridge.

191 Douglas Water

Contributors Peter Brown, Ian Thom and

Neil Farmer

Grade 3/4(5+)

Length 1.5km

Start NS 328 978 (56.1431, -4.6934)

Finish NS 345 980 (56.1455, -4.6657)

Introduction

The Douglas flows into the west side of Loch Lomond, to the north of Glen Luss. The river starts off flat before dropping into a wooded valley. The rapids are plentiful and inspection easy. The Falls of Douglas, which mark the end of the run, are an impressive yet serious piece of water.

Gauge

If the river below the A82 road bridge looks just runnable, then this river is worth doing; if it is high and in flood, take care. If the gauge at the put-in shows 470, it is low but runnable. On the downstream side of the take-out bridge, if the midstream rock is just covered, the run is low.

Access

Turn off the A82 onto the Glen Douglas road. Almost immediately you will reach a small area next to a bridge crossing the Douglas. You can park your car here and walk upstream for a couple of minutes to see the Falls of Douglas. This is the take-out.

The put-in is about 1½ miles upstream at a lay-by where there is a track down to the weir. There are alternate access points further up the glen, but be aware of the MOD land.

Description

The mini-gorge immediately below the weir is technically the hardest section (except for the falls) and is worth scouting from the left bank. Beyond, as the river drops into the gorge, the fun continues, with many short grade 3/4
rapids. These are all easily inspected, often from the last eddy above them. Fallen trees are a potential hazard making inspection worthwhile. One narrow grade 4 about a kilometre into the run is particularly prone to tree blockages and is usually inspected from the right. Grade 3 water continues until a longer rapid is encountered. This consists of some 100m of grade 3+ water, ending in a nice slab slide after a left turn. From here, a few straightforward rapids
take you down to the egress, immediately above the Falls of Douglas. Don’t miss the eddy! The falls have been paddled, but not at a level where the rest of the river would go. A short walk is required to get back to the car park by the bridge. Take care as the return path is often slippery with a steep drop down to the river.

Other important points

The description given is for medium levels. In high water, this river has a fast alpine feel to it. New surf waves appear, new lines are possible and eddies are fewer, an incredible run. It is strongly advised to inspect the last eddy above the falls before paddling the river. At certain levels, this eddy is small, and the lead in to the falls is not obvious from the river.

192 Luss Water

Contributor Neil Farmer

Grade 4(4+)

Length 3km

Start NS 331 943 (56.1121, -4.6858)

Finish NS 358 926 (56.0976, -4.6413)

Introduction

The Luss flows from the west into the bottom end of Loch Lomond. This is a small river in a deep wooded glen. It has many paddleable
rapids, but take care to miss out the Falls of Luss.

Gauge

The river needs plenty of water, but is a serious undertaking at very high flows. Most of the rocks below the A82 road bridge should be covered. 0.5 on the SEPA gauge should be considered a minimum flow.

Access

Turn off the A82 and drive to the top of Glen Luss. Put in on the Mollochan Burn, which should look small but runnable. Parking is limited here – there is alternative parking down the glen below Glenmollochan Farm. The take-out is below the old road bridge, in Luss village, where there is a large car park.

Description

Most of the rapids are runnable at grade 4. Watch out for the 3m, two-tier fall on the Mollochan Burn, which appears some 200m from the start. The harder rapids (Gd 4+) on the main river are in the first kilometre. After 1.5km the river drops into a nasty tree choked gorge, The Falls of Luss, which can be portaged either side. The river then eases slightly on the way down to the village of Luss.

Other important points

This run is similar in style to the neighbouring Douglas Water and can be choked with trees in places. The Upper Luss, above the section described, has a few grade 4 drops, interspersed with flat water, but would require a walk-in.

Further south, the Fruin Water, South Western Loch Lomond (NS 320 846; 56.0247, -4.6972) has been run giving a section of grade 4 white water with significant tree hazard. It needs a lot of water.
