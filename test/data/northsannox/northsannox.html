277 North Sannox Burn

(Isle of Arran)

Contributor Brendan Emery

Grade 4/5

Length 1km

Start NR 985 468 (55.6731, -5.2054)

Finish NR 992 468 (55.6731, -5.1933)

Introduction

If you find yourself on the Isle of Arran during the winter with lots of rain and your creek boat on the roof, there are many runs similar in nature to the North Sannox Burn. They demand a major walk-in, but your efforts will be well rewarded.

The North Sannox Burn cascades down from the slopes of Coire nan Ceum on the north-east side of the island and finally flows out into Sannox Bay and the Firth of Clyde. The burn gives a narrow rock slab feel and has you peering over every horizon line from start to finish.

Gauge

This run is most suitable after prolonged heavy rain, as it drops off in matter of hours. The burn should look bank full at the hillwalkers’ car park.

Access

You can reach the Isle of Arran by catching the ferry from Ardrossan in Ayrshire to the main ferry terminal in the town of Brodick, or from Claonaig on the Kintyre peninsular to Lochranza at the north end of the island. For ferry crossing and timetables go to www.calmac.co.uk .

To reach the take-out, drive north out of Brodick on the A841 (or south from Lochranza). As you continue north through the village of Sannox, you will get your first view of the burn on your right. Turn left just before the North Sannox Bridge and park in the hillwalkers’ car park. Take out just before the river goes under the road bridge. To get to the put-in, get your boat on your back and follow the trail on river right. Keep walking until you reach the north edge of the forest, or put in at any point on the walk up, depending on how brave you are feeling and the water level.

Description

This is a gem of a steep burn that leaves you wanting more. The burn demands respect in high flows, so inspect on the walk up and get your camera out ready for action. The main section is normally run from an obvious double drop just inside the forest tree line. Above this section, there are three more drops in the upper forest, possibly grade 4/5, but there is little information on how runnable these drops are when the river is in condition.

Put in above the double drop, Nut Cracker. The first 4m drop is steep and split into two channels; run on river right into the plunge pool and then you’re straight into the second 6m slab with a cushy landing, giving you enough time to catch your breath. At lower flows, you can drop into the pool between both drops and run the second drop, as the first will be scrapey and unenticing.

The river narrows in and picks up pace very quickly. The next 200m are pool drop in character before you hit the next horizon line, Corkscrew Slab. Prepare for ludicrous acceleration as you head over the horizon, left of centre, and twist steeply right and over the final flume into the shallow, but safe, pool below. The burn narrows up once again and the paddler is met by more enjoyable read and run slabs. Jump out here and run the main section again, or if you are still a jabbering wreck, continue down through the bouncy grade 3 open glen rapids to the take-out at the car park.

Other important points

If the river is chocolate brown and in flood, it would be wise to scout all the drops on the walk-in to assess how feasible it is. At this flow the river becomes very continuous with few eddies and is grade 5. If your ass is twitching like a rabbit’s nose, best to put in lower down or leave it for another day!

The excellent path alongside provides an easy walk-in and plenty of photo opportunities for soaked through, and surely jealous, non-paddling friends.

On the east side of Arran, the larger Machrie Water has a good section of 3/4 with a couple of challenging falls, from Glaister Bridge (NR 928 340; 55.5558, -5.2855) down to Machrie Bridge (NR 894 332; 55.5470, -5.3384).

Burns Country

This area is popular with paddlers from the Central Belt, though perhaps unfairly it’s often considered to play ‘second fiddle’ to a trip into the Highlands. Easily accessible from Carlisle and the M6, it is also a popular destination for those visiting from across the border. The rivers described here are in many ways the local paddlers’ pick of the bunch. Many other great runs exist; take a look at the Galloway Rivers Facebook page for some videos http://gallowayrivers.wixsite.com/gallowayrivers. Playboaters will find something of value on the Nith at almost all levels but for most other runs you will need to wait for rain.

278 Doon

 

(Ness Glen)

Contributors Neil Farmer and Alex Lumsden

Grade 3

Length 1km

Start NS 476 015 (55.2843, -4.3996)

Finish NS 477 027 (55.2951, -4,3999)

Introduction

This river flows from Loch Doon near Dalmellington, in a north-westerly direction, to join the Firth of Clyde near Ayr. The Ness Glen section runs from the dam at Loch Doon. To get the most out of this run it’s worth running a few laps of the first kilometre.

Gauge

The dam at Loch Doon releases a constant compensation flow of 2.6 cumecs, which gives enough water to paddle the Ness Glen section throughout the year. This stretch is not recommended when there’s a lot of water around due to the serious tree hazard. The flow is occasionally slightly increased and if there appears to be ample water at the put-in, inspecting the run below the small footbridge would be sensible.

Access

Drive south along the A713, through Dalmellington. After the town, take the second right, signposted to Loch Doon. Drive over the dam and park on the left. Follow the path on the river left. A quick walk back up the glen to get your car is easy enough or you can drive down to get your boat by following the track that branches to the left about a kilometre from the dam. If you are looking for a longer trip it is possible to continue to the B714 road bridge after crossing Bogton Loch (NS 462 059; 55.3238, -4.4250).

Description

The run starts below the dam. The weir 40m downstream is often run in the middle, but has seriously damaged many a boat! (Portage right or start your trip below the weir.) What follows is tight, steep and enjoyable. All of the rapids are grade 3 and can be inspected, but be very careful of tree blockages. Care should be taken and paddlers should be confident of making breakouts. There is a small pool towards the end of the run where the river takes a left and widens. Take out river left to do the run again, or paddle a further 300m downstream and take out at the low bridge where there is parking available.
