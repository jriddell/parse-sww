318 Hermitage Water

 

 

Contributors Mark Watson and Corey Watson

Grade 3/3+

Length 3km

Start NY 474 968 (55.2633, -2.8280)

Finish NY 474 968 (55.2552, -2.7901)

Introduction

A rare treasure, the run will depend on the
level you catch it at. It comes up quickly and runs off just as fast, so having a plan B for your day’s paddling is advised.

Gauge

The Hermitage Water@Braidlie SEPA gauge needs 0.9 or over for it to run.

Access

Get in just below a small stone bridge near Billhope Cottage where a water-gate crosses the river.

Description

The river can be inspected on the drive up. Once you’re on, there are no major features just lots of smaller ledges, holes and wave trains with overhead branches to keep you busy. Take out after Hermitage Castle (which is worth a look as it’s steeped in history). Choose your take-out depending on group size. This small river will not take a large group, and it’s worth splitting up if you have more than three or four paddlers. At higher levels the run will be grade 3+ due to the overhanging branches and probability of tree issues.

Other important points

You can inspect on the drive up, as the road follows the river.

Appendices – River Names and Pronunciation

River Names and Pronunciation

By Ron Cameron

River and place names in Scotland come from a variety of languages spoken here at one time or another. Many come from Gaelic which was widely spoken except in the South East. Norse names are common especially in the north and the islands. Brythonic names (Pictish and Welsh) are ancient and widely distributed. Anglian, Scots and English names dominate in the South East but are common elsewhere. Some names come from really ancient pre-Celtic Indo-European base languages. River names are usually very old and may not resemble any modern language. Although hazardous to attempt a translation, as names often come to resemble something completely different, the meanings I offer here are either direct translations, likely meanings or copied from published sources. For more translations and a full list of the sources used, visit www.andyjacksonfund.org.uk

Phonetic guides to Gaelic pronunciation are approximate as the Gaelic language uses a variety of sound that are not present in English. In the phonetics guide ch is always as in loch, rather than church. Ron Cameron is solely to blame for the phonetics, which are shown below in square brackets, [phonetics].

Recurring words:

Aber (Pictish and Welsh) River mouth

Inbhir (Gaelic) River mouth [eenvir sometimes eenyir] Anglicised to Inver

Esk (Pictish, Welsh and Brythonic) River

Abhainn (Gaelic) River [aveen or locally aween]

Allt (Gaelic) Stream or burn [owlt]

Avon (Scotland and England) River or Afon (Welsh and Pictish)

Eas (Gaelic) Waterfall [ess]

Fèith (Gaelic) Boggy stream [fay]

Gill (Norse) Burn / small stream

Glais (Archaic Gaelic) River [glash]

Uisge (Gaelic) Water [ooshka] Sometimes used for a watercourse

Srath (Gaelic) Strath / glen / valley.
